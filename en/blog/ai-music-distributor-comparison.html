Platform Notes

DistroKid: Good for frequent releases, but calculate the extras

DistroKid is attractive because of its annual upload model, especially for creators releasing many singles. But the real cost is not just the membership fee. Social Media Pack, Content ID, Leave a Legacy, cover licensing, and other extras can change the long-term economics.

LANDR: Strong for tool integration and catalog continuity

LANDR is appealing for creators who already value its ecosystem and want a lower-friction distribution route. A key point is that releases can remain live after subscription cancellation, but royalties may move into a commission model. Chinese-language creators should pay attention to Apple Music title limitations.

RouteNote: Low-cost market testing

RouteNote Free is useful when you are still testing audience reaction, release workflow, and platform behavior. The main benefit is low upfront cost; the trade-off is revenue share. For early AI music creators, it can be a lower-risk testing route.

TuneCore / UnitedMasters: Better for Content ID and social monetization decisions

If YouTube, TikTok, Instagram, or Facebook usage tracking matters to you, do not only ask whether a distributor offers Content ID. You need to check eligibility, fees, revenue share, platform coverage, and whether AI-assisted or AI-generated content qualifies.

The 5 Questions AI Music Creators Should Ask

  1. Is my work AI-assisted or highly AI-generated?
  2. How many songs will I release each year?
  3. Do I need my catalog to stay live after I stop paying?
  4. Do I need YouTube / TikTok / Meta Content ID or UGC monetization?
  5. Does my music include Chinese titles or target Chinese-language markets?

  • Frequent releases: start by comparing DistroKid, TuneCore, LANDR, and Amuse.
